# Title: Everyone deserves to enjoy Video Games: using Visual Language Models for accessibility (1 Hour)

My friend's daughter loves Stardew Valley. But the way her vision works, she can't see the items clearly when they appear in treasure chests or her inventory. I wanted to help her enjoy playing the game rather than getting frustrated trying to figure out what goodies were in the treasure chest. Turns out, advances in Vision Language Models (VLMs) make this a tractable problem I could take on. We built a simple pipeline: she takes a screenshot on her iPad, sends it to a visual language model, gets back a text description of what's on screen, and hears it read aloud. Suddenly those treasure chests made sense. The game became less frustrating for her. This talk is about how you can take what she and I learned to leverage the power of Visual Language Models for other use cases. It's about how to use these tools to expand accessibility in new and exciting ways. In this session, we'll explore what visual language models actually are and why they're powerful for this kind of problem. We'll walk through our specific implementation: the decisions we made, the models we tested, and why some worked better than others. Most importantly, you'll see the working application and dig into the actual code. We'll talk through what we learned—what surprised us, what didn't work, and where we hit the edge cases. By the end, you'll understand not just how we solved this accessibility problem, but the shape of similar problems you might solve. You'll leave seeing it in action, a Github repo of code, and a HuggingFace repo for our models which means you can go find your own applications of this work. Come see how visual AI can expand what's possible for the people using the software you build.
